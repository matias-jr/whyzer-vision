The Three Filtering Layers

Three layers.
Different rules.

Most sellers try to solve all three problems with the same tactic - better copywriting. That fails because each layer judges your email on completely different criteria. You need to understand each one on its own terms.

Layer 1  Â· Pattern-based filtering - the behaviorally trained inbox

Tools like Gmail's Priority Inbox, Microsoft Outlook's Focused Inbox, Superhuman, and Shortwave learn the executive's behavior over time - who they reply to, what subjects they open, what they archive without reading. They build a predictive model: emails that match known patterns of relevance get surfaced, emails that match patterns of vendor outreach get buried. This layer is invisible, personal, and learned. It reflects the individual executive's engagement history, not a general rule.

Layer 2  Â· LLM triage - the AI that reads, scores, and summarizes your email

This is the faster-growing and less understood layer. Microsoft Copilot in Outlook now reads incoming emails and assigns each one a visible priority rating - High, Normal, or Low - with a written reason shown to the executive in the reading pane. Google's Gemini in Gmail is rolling out similar capabilities in 2026, starting with AI Overviews that summarize threads and an AI Inbox that surfaces priority items and suggested to-dos. The LLM reads your actual words. An email that reads as generic vendor outreach gets classified as exactly that. An email with specific financial context about the executive's business gets classified differently - and earns a higher rating.

Layer 3  Â· Agentic filtering - AI that acts on the executive's behalf (emerging)

Tools like Lindy.ai are beginning to offer autonomous inbox management that goes beyond triage. The AI can draft and send responses, route to team members, or silently archive based on instructions the executive has set. This layer is less common today but growing fast in enterprise environments. An email that fails layers one and two may never reach a human at all. This is where the market is heading - which means understanding layers one and two now is what prepares you for layer three.

What Each Layer Scores

What the machines
are actually evaluating

Once you understand what each filter is measuring, you can write directly to those signals - without making the email feel calculated or unnatural.

Layer 1 - Pattern-Based Filter Signals

These tools aren't reading your content. They're reading metadata and behavioral patterns. The signals that earn a higher priority score:

Prior thread historyLayer 1

Any prior reply from the executive - even a brief one - dramatically raises your future email priority. A warm introduction that generates even a two-word reply changes your sender status permanently.

Sender domain reputationLayer 1

Emails from recognized company domains with proper SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authentication score higher. Sending from a high-volume outreach platform or a generic domain can suppress delivery before the filter even evaluates content.

Subject line pattern matchLayer 1

"Quick chat," "following up," "synergy," and "partnership opportunity" match suppressed patterns across millions of users. Specific subjects referencing the executive's actual business context break the pattern.

Absence of bulk indicatorsLayer 1

Tracking pixels, unsubscribe links, shared sending IPs, and sequence-platform signatures all signal mass outreach. One-to-one emails from a personal work inbox with no tracking score significantly higher.

Layer 2 - LLM Content Evaluation Signals

This layer reads your actual words and forms a judgment about relevance. The signals that earn a high priority rating from Copilot and a to-do extraction from Gmail's AI Inbox:

Specific financial contextLayer 2

Referencing the executive's actual business - a recent earnings result, a stated strategic priority, a market condition affecting their sector - signals the email was written for this person. The LLM detects this as genuine relevance, not templated outreach.

Named executive contextLayer 2

Referencing the executive's stated mandate or recent public statements grounds the email in their reality. Copilot evaluates relevance against the recipient's known context - pulling from across their Microsoft 365 environment.

Clear, extractable actionLayer 2

Gmail's AI Inbox surfaces emails as to-dos only when they contain a clear, actionable request. Copilot scores emails with a specific ask more favorably. "Do you have 15 minutes?" generates nothing. A specific question tied to a named business problem is extractable.

Absence of vendor languageLayer 2

"Industry-leading," "excited to connect," "help you hit your KPIs," "cutting-edge solution" - these phrases pattern-match to sales noise instantly. The AI's summary will reflect exactly this, earning a Low priority tag.

Writing for the LLM Summary - Not Just the Human

Copilot and Gemini generate a two-sentence summary of your email before the executive reads it. That summary is written top-down - the AI extracts its headline from your first two lines, not from your best line buried in paragraph three. Structure your email accordingly: put your strongest, most specific financial claim first. One claim per sentence. Short paragraphs. The ask at the end. Your email will be summarized before it is read. The summary is what gets the executive to open it.

What About Links and LLM-Readable Pages?

Including a link in your email does not improve your Layer 2 priority score - Copilot and Gmail's AI Inbox evaluate the email body itself, not linked pages. However, two things happen after you get through the filter that a good link can influence. First, if the executive clicks through, they land somewhere. A company-specific POV page built around their financial reality reinforces your credibility and extends the narrative you started. A generic product homepage resets it. Second, executives and their assistants are increasingly using AI tools - Perplexity, ChatGPT, Gemini - to research vendors before responding to outreach. A link to a structured, specific, financially grounded page performs better in that secondary research loop than anything generic. The link doesn't bypass the filter. It deepens the quality of what happens if you get through.

The Rules

Nine rules for
emails that get through

The Framework
  • 1
    Lead with their financial reality, not your product. The first sentence should reference something specific and financially relevant to their business - a market condition, a number from their earnings, a strategic priority they've stated publicly. This is the single most important signal for all three filtering layers.
  • 2
    Write a subject line that could only apply to this company. If your subject line could appear in an email sent to 500 people, it will be treated as mass outreach. "Q3 P1 incident cost - 3 questions" performs better than "Improving observability at [Company]" because it could only be written for a company with a Q3 P1 incident problem.
  • 3
    Remove every phrase an LLM has seen a million times. Audit your email for: "excited to connect," "industry-leading," "quick chat," "reaching out because," "impressed by your growth," "help you hit your KPIs," "cutting-edge," "synergies." These phrases signal vendor noise to Layer 2 regardless of everything else in your email.
  • 4
    Make one ask. Make it extractable. Gmail's AI Inbox only surfaces emails as to-dos when it can extract a clear action. Copilot assigns higher priority when there's a specific, bounded ask. "Worth 20 minutes to validate whether the exposure is in the range your team already knows about?" is extractable. "Would love to find time to chat" is not.
  • 5
    Send from your personal work email with no tracking infrastructure. Tracking pixels, shared sending domains, unsubscribe footers, and sequence-platform signatures are visible signals of mass outreach to Layer 1 filters. The highest-performing executive outreach looks like a one-to-one message - because it is.
  • 6
    Warm the executive across channels before your email lands. The goal is to be a known entity before you arrive in their inbox - not a stranger triggering a cold outreach pattern. This isn't about which channel comes first. It's about genuine multi-channel presence: a thoughtful comment on their LinkedIn post, a phone call that references their public priorities, a voicemail that previews what your email will say. Each touch that generates any response - a reply, a call back, a connection acceptance - raises your sender status when the email eventually arrives. The channel doesn't matter. The accumulated familiarity does.
  • 7
    Structure your email body for LLM extraction, not just human reading. The LLM triage tools - Copilot, Gemini - read your plain text and extract a two-sentence summary to show the executive. Write with that extraction in mind. One financial claim per sentence. Short paragraphs. The most important signal in the first two lines, not buried in paragraph three. Your email is being summarized before it is read. Make the summary earn a high-priority tag.
  • 8
    Include a link - but make it earn its place. A link in your email doesn't help you get past the triage filter - Copilot and Gmail don't follow links when scoring priority. But if the executive opens your email and clicks through, where they land matters. A Whyzer-built POV page specific to their company reinforces the financial narrative in your email. It also serves a secondary AI interaction: executives and their assistants increasingly prompt tools like Perplexity or ChatGPT to research vendors before responding. A link to a structured, specific, financially grounded page performs better in that research loop than a generic product page. The link doesn't bypass the filter. It deepens the quality of the conversation if you get through.
  • 9
    Frame it as a hypothesis to validate, not a product to pitch. "I have a view I'd like to validate with you" performs better than "I'd love to show you how we can help." The first positions the executive as the authority on their own business. The second positions you as a vendor seeking a platform. All three filter layers register this difference.

Pre-Send Checklist

Before you hit send

Run every executive outreach email through this checklist. If you answer "no" to more than two, rewrite before sending.

  • Does the first sentence reference their specific financial reality?

    A number, a market condition, a stated priority, a recent result - something that could only be true for this company right now.

  • Could this subject line only apply to this company?

    If you can swap in another company name and send it unchanged, the subject is too generic.

  • Have you removed all vendor language?

    Search for "excited," "reaching out," "industry-leading," "quick chat," "KPIs," "synergy," "cutting-edge." Remove every instance.

  • Is there exactly one ask - and is it extractable?

    One ask, clearly stated, tied to a specific business reason. Phrased as a question the AI can pull out as a to-do.

  • Is it under 150 words?

    Shorter emails with a clear signal score better across all three layers. If you need more than 150 words, the financial context isn't specific enough yet.

  • Are you sending from your personal work email, not a sequencing tool?

    No tracking pixel. No unsubscribe link. No shared IP. One-to-one send only.

  • Is your most important financial claim in the first two lines?

    The LLM summarizes your email from the top down. If the signal is buried in paragraph three, it may not make the summary. Put your strongest, most specific point first.

  • If you included a link, does it lead somewhere specific - not a generic homepage?

    A link to a company-specific POV page or financial narrative reinforces your credibility if they click through, and performs better if they or their assistant research you with an AI tool afterward. A link to your homepage undermines both.

  • Could Copilot summarize this as "contextually relevant outreach"?

    If the two-sentence AI summary would read "vendor requesting meeting," rewrite. If it reads "sender references specific financial situation and requests validation meeting," you're ready to send.
