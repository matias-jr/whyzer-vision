The example

The "value beyond seats" narrative. Modeled.

Five sections. Built for the page that survives procurement's first question. Left column is the narrative. Right column is what each paragraph is doing strategically.

Treat it as a template, not a script. The fictional company is "Atlas" — a customer support platform defending its renewal at "Acme Co."

Take seats off the table 1 of 5
01 · Opening frame

Redraw the line before procurement does.

— the narrative

Acme Co. is paying for Atlas to do something specific: keep your support tickets resolved fast enough that your customer satisfaction stays above 90% and your renewal rate stays above 95%. The math on seats doesn't measure either one. The math on outcomes does.

What this paragraph is doing

Opening move: take the seat conversation off the table before it starts. Sellers who let procurement open with seat count have already lost the meeting.

This sentence redraws the line — the buyer is paying for an outcome, not a login count. Everything that follows is built on that ground.

Build the case 3 of 5
02 · What we deliver

Three things no seat count can measure.

— the narrative

Atlas delivers three things no per-seat math can capture.

Fourteen prebuilt integrations across Salesforce, Zendesk, Slack, and Snowflake — each one a multi-month engineering project before we showed up.

The escalation playbooks built around your top 20 enterprise accounts, with the SLA logic your operations team designed into the product. That logic is your institutional memory, now running automatically.

The named CSM hours and quarterly business reviews that drove the playbook updates that lifted your CSAT by six points over the last 18 months.

What this paragraph is doing

Three concrete deliverables that have nothing to do with login count.

The CFO can argue with seat counts all day. They can't argue with integrations they don't have time to rebuild, or with a CSAT lift they can verify on their own dashboard.

Specificity is the work here. "Workflows and integrations" is air. "Fourteen prebuilt integrations across [named systems]" is leverage.

03 · The AI question, answered first

Bring the displacement question forward.

— the narrative

You will hear this quarter that AI agents can replace the work your support reps are doing — and that Atlas's value goes down as your headcount goes down.

The honest answer: your headcount may decrease. The work doesn't disappear. The integrations, the playbooks, and the data layer Atlas maintains don't get cheaper when your team gets smaller. They get more important. The fewer humans on your team, the more your tooling has to work without supervision.

What this paragraph is doing

Don't wait for the CFO to ask. Bring the question forward and answer it before procurement does.

This paragraph turns the AI displacement story from a threat into a reason your product matters more, not less. It also signals to the buyer that you've already thought through the risk they were about to raise — which is the move that earns trust.

04 · The switching cost, in real numbers

Give procurement a number, not a theory.

— the narrative

The cost of replacing Atlas isn't the contract price. It's the four to six months of rebuild time, the data migration risk, the loss of integration history, and the team retraining while your customer SLAs continue uninterrupted.

Conservative estimate, based on the last switch a comparable customer ran: $1.4M over 12 months. The seat-count savings from leaving Atlas would have to clear that bar before they're a real win.

What this paragraph is doing

Don't give the CFO "switching cost" as theory. Give them a number — sourced, defensible, specific.

This is also where you preempt the cheaper-vendor pitch before it lands. If procurement has already gotten a quote from a competitor, this paragraph is the wall that quote runs into.

Close the loop 1 of 5
05 · The forward commitment

Close on confidence. Put the CFO in the room.

— the narrative

For the renewal, we are proposing two changes.

First, a value structure that ties our pricing to outcomes your team already tracks — ticket resolution time, CSAT, renewal rate. Second, a quarterly review with your COO and CFO where we walk through those numbers together.

The seat count becomes a footnote. The conversation becomes about what Atlas is doing for the metrics that move your business.

What this paragraph is doing

Close on confidence. Don't leave the buyer thinking "maybe." Tell them what the next 12 months looks like, and put the COO and CFO in the room.

Getting higher in the account before procurement reorganizes it is the Mega Deal Secrets move. This is where the writing earns its keep.
