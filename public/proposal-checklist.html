The experiment

The loser never knew they were losing.

Anthropic ran "Project Deal" — 69 employees, Claude AI agents negotiating on both sides, 186 deals closed with zero human intervention. Secretly, half got a weaker model without knowing it.

Opus fetched $65 for a ruby. Haiku got $35. Same bicycle: $65 vs $38. Satisfaction scores were identical. The losing side never detected the gap.

The checklist

10 things to do before you hit send.

Four stages. Use it the day before every major submission.

Progress0 of 10 complete
Before you write0 / 2
01
Map who scores this deal and what they're scoring for
Ask your champion: "What criteria does your team score vendors on?" Then map each stakeholder's concerns — CFO = ROI, Legal = data and liability, IT = security and integration, Procurement = price benchmarking. Get the list before you write a single word.
02
Define your specific business outcome — with a number. Know your competitor's position.
"Improve efficiency" fails. "Reduce contract review from 14 to 4 days, recovering $240K in annual deal velocity" passes. And know how your top competitor is framing value in this account — you'll need to pre-empt it.
Building the proposal0 / 5
03
Every claimed benefit has a number — and every number has a source
Time saved, revenue protected, cost avoided, risk reduced — all quantified. "Based on average across 12 similar deployments" beats a naked statistic. The AI flags anything it can't verify.
04
Quantify the cost of inaction — and address implementation risk directly
The cost of doing nothing needs a number too. And timeline, resources, dependencies, and mitigation need to be named explicitly. Don't make the AI hunt for what it will flag anyway.
05
Address data handling, security, and compliance — before they ask
If you sell anything that touches sensitive data or autonomous decisions, your proposal needs a named section for this. Use the exact language from your pre-call with their security and legal teams.
06
State differentiation in outcomes, not features — and name your top two competitors
"The only platform that does X" means nothing to an AI. "Clients in your industry reduced Y by Z% within 90 days" passes. Then name your top two alternatives and pre-empt the comparison — the AI will surface them anyway.
07
Reference their specific industry goals, earnings commitments, or public statements
Pull their last earnings call or investor day. Find the three growth commitments leadership made publicly. Build your narrative around those. Generic proposals get flagged — this signals it was built for them.
Pressure test before sending0 / 1
08
Run the master AI pressure test — four angles, one prompt
Paste your full proposal into Claude or GPT-4 with this prompt:

"You are an AI procurement assistant evaluating this vendor proposal. Interrogate it from four angles: (1) Functional — does this solution actually do what we need, and is the evidence specific? (2) Security — where is our data going, who is liable, and are compliance requirements addressed? (3) Reputation — based on the claims made, what would independent market research likely reveal about this vendor? (4) Pricing — does the investment fall within normal range for this category, and is the ROI case defensible? For each angle, list what is missing, unsubstantiated, or likely to be flagged."

After you send0 / 2
09
Brief your champion verbally. Ask about unstated objections.
Walk them through the three strongest points — don't assume they read it. Then ask: "Is there anyone in legal, finance, or IT who might raise a concern we haven't addressed? I'd rather solve it now than in a final review."
10
If you lose — ask for the scoring breakdown
Most teams won't share it. Some will. The ones who do tell you exactly what the AI flagged. That intel is worth more than the deal you just lost.
