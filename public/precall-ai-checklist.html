Four moves. Ten minutes the morning of any first meeting. You walk in as the verifier — holding the same picture your buyer holds of you, plus the moves to redraw it on your terms.

01 · Ask AI what they’re asking

Get their words. Not your guesses.

Your buyer searched you with AI before they ever met you — in their language, not yours. These prompts are written as the executive would write them. Paste each one, in order, into ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity.

How to do it

1. The category scan

I'm a [BUYER TITLE] at a [INDUSTRY] company looking to solve [PROBLEM]. Who are the three vendors I should evaluate first, and what is each one best at?

2. The price reality check

What's the realistic all-in cost of solving [PROBLEM] at a company my size? Include subscription, implementation, and ongoing — and tell me where vendors typically hide costs.

3. The shortlist verdict

If you had to pick between [COMPANY] and its top two competitors for a [INDUSTRY] company solving [PROBLEM], who would you recommend — and what would push you away from [COMPANY]?

4. The hidden risk

What’s the most common reason projects in this category fail at companies my size, even when the vendor is good?

5. The market signal

What are [BUYER TITLE]s in [INDUSTRY] saying about [COMPANY] right now — in LinkedIn posts, podcasts, or recent interviews?
✓
After step 01You know what the AI already told your buyer about you, your price, your competitors, and your market — in your buyer’s own words.

02 · Find their main priority

The number on their scorecard.

The AI can describe what you do. It can’t tie it to the goal this executive is personally graded on. So you do that. Find the number, then confirm it in the first five minutes of the meeting.

Where to look (in this order)

  • Earnings calls. Search “[Company] earnings call transcript” — read the CEO’s prepared remarks and any Q&A about your buyer’s function.
  • Shareholder / investor letter. Annual or quarterly. The CEO writes about the year’s priorities in their own language.
  • 10-K or annual report. Go straight to the “Strategic Priorities” or MD&A section.
  • The executive’s LinkedIn. Their last 90 days of posts and reactions tell you what they’re thinking about.
  • Recent press releases. What has the company committed to publicly this year?

Be specific

Bad: “They want to improve efficiency.”

Good: “They want to cut time-to-close from 90 days to 45 by end of FY.”

Write down the exact metric, in their language — with a number and a deadline if you can find one.

Verify it in the first five minutes of the meeting

“Before we get into our side of it, I’d guess you’ve already seen what we do online. So let me skip that and go straight to the part the internet can’t tell you: how this connects to the goal I imagine you’re being measured on this year. Mind if I check whether I’ve got that goal right?”
✓
After step 02You walk in knowing the specific number on the executive’s scorecard — and you confirm it in the room before you pitch anything.

03 · Introduce your approach (with the reframe)

Make your approach the obvious next step.

You don’t introduce your product. You introduce an approach — framed as the path to the goal they just confirmed. Done right, you change what’s being evaluated: from “is this vendor any good” to “is this the right approach for my goal.”

Research what they’ve already tried

You don’t want to suggest something they tried two years ago and it bombed. Find out:

  • Press releases. Past initiatives related to the same problem — especially ones that quietly disappeared.
  • Job postings — current and last 18 months. Tells you which teams they’ve built around this problem.
  • The buyer’s team on LinkedIn. What have their direct reports been working on?
  • Earnings calls. Listen for “we’ve been working on…” or “we’re still working through…” on this topic.

Build the reframe

The conventional frame: Most teams frame this problem as A, B, and C — the obvious dimensions.

The contrarian truth: The real driver is actually X, and it sits inside one of those (usually C).

The approach that helps: Y — your solution, framed as the answer to X.

Write your three lines now, in plain executive language. If you can’t name X without your product on the page, your reframe isn’t sharp yet.

Use it in the meeting (right after they share the goal)

“Got it. From what you’ve told me, and from what we’ve seen with previous customers, you might be framing this problem as [A, B, and C]. What we’ve actually found is the opposite — the real driver is [X], sitting inside [C]. And the approach that helps is [Y, your solution].”
✓
After step 03You walk in with a fresh frame on their problem — one that turns your approach into the natural next step instead of one option among three.

04 · Surface competitors and find your leverage

Where they’re strong. Where the goal isn’t.

When the competitor comes up — and they will — you don’t fight on features. You name what they’re strong at honestly, and then point to the part of the buyer’s goal they don’t move. That’s your leverage.

Tie the leverage to a time-bound outcome

Use it in the meeting (when the competitor surfaces)

“Yes, they’re strong on that. But based on the goal we just discussed, here’s an approach we’ve used before — one that solves [Y] and helps you hit your goal by [time].”

Put it together

The full script.

Strung together in a real meeting, the moves fire in this order:

Opening — frame the conversation about them

“Before we get into our side of it, I’d guess you’ve already seen what we do online. So let me skip that and go straight to the part the internet can’t tell you: how this connects to the goal I imagine you’re being measured on this year. Mind if I check whether I’ve got that goal right?”

The reframe — right after they share the goal

“Got it. From what you’ve told me, and from what we’ve seen with previous customers, you might be framing this problem as [A, B, and C]. What we’ve actually found is the opposite — the real driver is [X], sitting inside [C]. And the approach that helps is [Y, your solution].”

When the competitor surfaces — usually mid-meeting

“Yes, they’re strong on that. But based on the goal we just discussed, here’s an approach we’ve used before — one that solves [Y] and helps you hit your goal by [time].”

By the end, the goal is the center of the room, your approach is the lens, and the comparison question is behind you. That’s the verifier in action.
